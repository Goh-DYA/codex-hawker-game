## Framing

The setting is a fictional, contemporary community dining space inspired by Singapore hawker-centre practices. Hawker culture is treated as shared living heritage and a multicultural social space, not a generic “Asian food” theme or backdrop.

Authoritative starting sources:

- [UNESCO: Hawker culture in Singapore](https://ich.unesco.org/en/RL/hawker-culture-in-singapore-community-dining-and-culinary-practices-in-a-multicultural-urban-context-01568)
- [National Heritage Board Roots: Built for Great Taste](https://www.roots.gov.sg/stories-landing/stories/our-hawker-culture-built-for-great-taste)
- [National Heritage Board Roots: Hawker Culture material heritage](https://www.roots.gov.sg/stories-landing/stories/Hawker-Culture)
- [NEA: Tray Return Programme](https://www.nea.gov.sg/media/news/news/index/nea-launches-tray-return-programme-to-keep-our-hawker-centres-clean)
- [NEA: Keep Eating Places Clean and Safe](https://www.nea.gov.sg/docs/default-source/our-services/public-cleanliness/covid-19/keep-eating-places-clean-and-safe-english.pdf)
- [MUIS: Singapore halal certification](https://www.muis.gov.sg/halal/about/)

These sources guide research; they do not substitute for practitioner/community review or grant rights to source images, logos, text, or recordings.

## Current design commitments

- Use original fictional stall names, symbols, layouts, UI, and art.
- Show communal seating, practical ventilation/lighting, menu boards, stall shutters, cleaning infrastructure, and tray-return facilities as functional systems.
- Represent Chinese, Malay, Indian, Peranakan, drinks, desserts, and vegetable-forward offerings without assigning customers by race or making one cuisine the default.
- Generate skin, hair, clothing, spending, patience, and preference independently.
- Use real dish names where accurate; write original descriptions and depictions.
- Do not reproduce a real centre, stall frontage, brand, certification mark, poster, uniform, or menu.
- Do not use accents, ethnic jokes, sacred imagery, flags, or scripts as decorative shorthand.
- Treat nutrition intents and health conditions as simplified needs for one visit, never as ethnic, age, disability, body-size, or socioeconomic traits. Do not imply prevalence, diagnosis, blame, or that any cuisine is inherently healthy or unhealthy.
- Review serving labels and every recipe-variant visual against recognizable local preparation while retaining the source-data uncertainty notice.

## High-risk labels and claims

Do not display halal, MUIS marks or lookalikes, vegetarian, vegan, allergy-safe, gluten-free, nut-free, or certification language without qualified review of the exact implementation. MUIS is the Singapore authority for halal certification, and its mark must not be imitated. In-game dietary tags are recipe/preference metadata only; the UI must say they are not real-world safety guidance.

Avoid invented translations. English is the launch language. Any Chinese, Malay, or Tamil environmental copy requires a named reviewer, source string, translation, back-translation or equivalent check, context screenshot, and approval record.

## Dish review queue

Every item in CONTENT_CATALOGUE.md needs a reviewer to check:

- name/spelling and whether a local qualifier is appropriate;
- cultural origin and cross-cultural influences without exclusive or false ownership;
- common ingredients, preparation, garnish, color, vessel, portion, and eating context;
- pork, alcohol, seafood, dairy, egg, nut, and other sensitive cues;
- whether “vegetarian” wording is supportable for stock, sauces, and preparation;
- whether grouping it under the proposed fictional stall is coherent;
- whether price/quality/progression framing creates a hierarchy between cuisines.
- whether nutrition comparison copy avoids moralizing dishes or presenting one cuisine as inherently healthier than another.

Priority review: ayam pongteh, babi pongteh, Nonya chap chye and laksa attribution, Indian-Muslim stall framing, Tamarind Leaf's vegetable/vegetarian wording, roti prata/thosai/idli presentation, nasi lemak accompaniments, Hokkien mee, char kway teow, Pick & Mix customisation, yong tau foo, thunder tea rice, popiah, bak kut teh, duck rice, kway chap, claypot chicken rice, seafood species, and all halal/dietary implications.

## Environment and behavior checklist

- [ ] Communal seats and table reservation behavior feel plausible without presenting “chope” as a joke or universal rule.
- [ ] Queue geometry, collection points, cleaners, tray return, bins, and dishwashing cues reflect current practice.
- [ ] Peak meal periods and customer flow are plausible.
- [ ] Stalls have practical shutters, menus, service openings, ventilation, and storage cues.
- [ ] Cleaning work is visible and dignified; tray return is not framed as eliminating cleaners.
- [ ] Customers show varied ages, clothing, mobility, group sizes, and routines without caricature.
- [ ] Food art and vessels are individually checked at gameplay scale.
- [ ] Decorative motifs are original and not a collage of decontextualized heritage symbols.
- [ ] No real address, merchant, logo, or protected visual identity appears.
- [ ] Tutorial copy explains the fictional nature of the centre.

## Required human review record

Before release, record reviewer name/role and relevant lived or professional experience, scope, build/version, date, findings, changes, unresolved disagreements, and sign-off or explicit non-approval. Ideally include at least one Singapore-based hawker-culture practitioner/researcher and reviewers across represented food traditions. Compensation and consent for review are a human production responsibility.
